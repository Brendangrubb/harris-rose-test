How they do it in

Centuries ago, tunnels were constructed beneath the town to store the wine while it fermented.

The chimneys provide ventilation.

Dig it deep and keep it cool.

Rosé, called rosado here, is made by cold macerating the skins together with the juice in a deposit for eight to 24 hours or until the right color tone from extraction of the skins is obtained. The winemaker makes the call.

By controlling the temperature, the fermentation does not begin. After this time the juice drains or is pressed away from the pips and skins and is moved to another deposit to begin fermenting. Almost always there is yeast added to inoculate, or jump-start, this process.

Cigales is a small farming community of 5,000 in western Spain. They’ve been making wine since before Ferdinand of Aragon disguised himself as a mule driver in an attempt to get close to Isabel the Catholic. Clarete is not red wine, but it’s not exactly rosé either. So, what is it?

The ecclesiastical icon of Cigales town is the Iglesia de Santiago Apostol, started in 1535 by Rodrigo Gil de Hontañon. Finished in 1772, its monumental white, limestone stones reveal the special position that Cigales held due to its proximity to the royal courts of Valladolid nearby. It regularly hosted counts from Benavente and kings and nobles from afar. It had residual star power. And they toasted with clarete.
